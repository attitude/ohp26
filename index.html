  1. 1. Veletržní palác – Národní galerie Praha

    Why: The single most important functionalist building in the country (1928, Tyl + Fuchs). When Le Corbusier visited in 1928 he allegedly said “I was astonished. I saw architecture, the architecture I've been thinking about.” Basically Prague's manifesto answer to everything on your list.

    Don't miss: The grand seven-story atrium with its glass-block floors. The original elevators and brass details. Inside: Mucha's Slav Epic and the Czech modern art collection (Kupka, Filla, Toyen).

  2. 2. Kongresové centrum Praha

    Why: The Palace of Culture (1981). Late-socialist brutalism at its most monumental — bronze, travertine, raw concrete, endless geometric repetition. The kind of naked-concrete wall you said you love, scaled up to a city block.

    Don't miss: The main 2,800-seat hall, the foyer's marble + bronze + glass cosmos, and the terrace view back across Vyšehrad and the Nusle valley.

  3. 3. Národní divadlo – provozní budova (Nová scéna)

    Why: Karel Prager's 1983 brutalist insertion next to the gold-roofed National Theatre. The dialogue between cast-concrete grid and the historic neo-renaissance is exactly the old/new tension you don't get to see often.

    Don't miss: The glass-brick façade of the Nová scéna (looks completely different lit from inside at dusk), Prager's exposed structural concrete in the back-of-house spaces, the rooftop.

  4. 4. Hotel Fairmont Golden Prague (ex-Intercontinental)

    Why: Karel Filsak's 1974 Intercontinental — Prague's most famous brutalist hotel, just reopened after the Fairmont conversion. Bronze, travertine, concrete relief panels. The whole 1970s government-luxury brutalism mood.

    Don't miss: Whatever original Filsak detailing survived the renovation — the lobby relief work, the bar levels, the riverside terraces. Open House is the rare moment you can see it without booking a room.

  5. 5. Palác Škodových závodů

    Why: Pavel Janák, 1926. Janák is the architect Czech modernism revolves around, and this is one of his cleanest functionalist works. Now Magistrát hl. m. Prahy.

    Don't miss: The grand staircase, the original elevator cores, the period office interiors that survived the conversion to city hall.

  6. 6. Trmalova vila

    Why: Your Wright pick. Jan Kotěra's 1903 villa — the father of Czech modern architecture absorbing exactly the Wright / Prairie school influences you fell for in Fallingwater. Built-in furniture, horizontal masses, integration with landscape.

    Don't miss: The original Kotěra-designed built-in furniture and lighting fixtures still in situ. This is essentially a Czech Robie House, in miniature.

  7. 7. Národní archiv a Státní oblastní archiv v Praze

    Why: The Chodovec archive complex — late socialist brutalism, finished 1990s but designed under normalization. Pure modular concrete, fortress-like volumes, the function-equals-form ethos applied to storing the nation's paper.

    Don't miss: The storage stacks tour (rarely open), the central reading room, the exterior modular concrete geometry from the access road.

  8. 8. Budova podniku zahraničního obchodu Strojimport

    Why: 1970s socialist-modernist headquarters of the state machinery export company. The kind of late-modernist corporate building Western Europe largely lost — Prague kept theirs.

    Don't miss: Original lobby interior, conference rooms, façade detailing. Period furniture and signage if it survived.

  9. 9. Areál Jinonice – Fakulta sociálních věd UK

    Why: A late-socialist university campus (originally a Communist Party training school), now Charles University's social-science faculty. Monumental concrete pavilions on an axial plan — utopian-brutalist campus planning.

    Don't miss: The axial spine between buildings, the lecture hall interiors, the rough concrete fenestration patterns.

  17. 17. Palác Adria

    Why: Pavel Janák + Josef Zasche, 1922–25. Not pure functionalism — this is Rondo-cubism, the brief Czech “national style” between cubism and functionalism. More decorative than your usual taste, but so specifically Czech avant-garde that an architecture lover shouldn't skip it.

    Don't miss: The internal passageway, the Adria theatre, the rooftop ceramic ornament.
